\section{Operators}\label{sec:operators}

In this section we introduce a family of semantic operators
  defined on \(\phi\)-expressions.
These operators do not extend the syntax of the calculus.
Instead, they describe systematic transformations of expressions that reveal,
  modify, or project their semantic content.
Each operator is defined in terms of the reduction semantics introduced earlier
  and preserves the core meaning of expressions
  while possibly changing their form or role.

\subsection{Contextualization}\label{sec:contextualization}

The \emph{contextualization} total function
  \(\mathbb{C} : \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{B} \to \mathcal{E}\)
  denoted as \( \ctx{e}{b} \), which removes \(\phiTerminal{\xi}\)
  from the expression,
  is defined by induction in \cref{fig:contextualization}.

\subsection{Normalization}\label{sec:normalization}

An expression that may be rewritten by the \emph{rules} (or \emph{reductions})
  listed in \cref{fig:reduction} is a \emph{reducible} expression.
The notation \(e_1 \trans e_2\), optionally followed by a condition,
  denotes a reduction of \(e_1\) to \(e_2\), if the condition holds.

These rules may be applied in any order.

A specific reduction may be denoted, for example,
  as \(\trans_{\nameref{r:dot}}\),
  or just \(\trans\) when no specific reduction is meant.
The notation \(e_1 \strans e_2\) denotes a reflexive transitive
  closure of all reductions, so that there is a possibly empty finite
  sequence of reductions between \(e_1\) and \(e_2\).

\begin{definition}[Normal Form]
An expression that has no more possible applications of reductions
  is \emph{irreducible} or a \emph{normal form}, denoted as \(\nf{}_i\)
  ranging over \(\mathcal{N} \subseteq \mathcal{E}\).
\end{definition}

Thus, \(\nf\) is a normal form of \(e\) if \(e \strans \nf\) and
  there is no expression \(e_1\) such that \(\nf \trans e_1\).

\begin{definition}[Absolute]
A normal form is \textbf{absolute},
  denoted by \(k\) and ranging over \(\mathcal{K} \subseteq \mathcal{N}\),
  if it is either
  \begin{inparaenum}[a)]
  \item $Q$,
  \item formation,
  \item dispatch with an absolute subject,
  or
  \item application with absolute subject and argument.
  \end{inparaenum}
\end{definition}

The \emph{normalization} function
  \(\mathbb{N} : \mathcal{E} \rightarrow \mathcal{N} \)
  maps expressions to normal forms.

The normalization function is total, meaning that
  every expression has a normal form.
The reduction relation terminates:
  for any \(e \in \mathcal{E}\) there is no infinite sequence of reductions,
  so finitely many steps always reach an irreducible \(\nf \in \mathcal{N}\).
Thus, \(\mathbb{N}\) is defined for every expression.

\ifdefined\examples
\Cref{app:normalization-examples} demonstrates how normalization
  works through examples.
\fi

The reduction relation is \emph{confluent}:
  if \(e \strans e_1\) and \(e \strans e_2\),
  then there exists an expression \(e_3\) such that
  \(e_1 \strans e_3\) and \(e_2 \strans e_3\).
Confluence guarantees that the normal form of an expression is unique,
  irrespective of the order in which the rules are applied.
We have proved this property mechanically in Lean~4,
  with the proof available in a public
  GitHub repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/objectionary/proof}}.

\subsection{Equivalence}\label{sec:equivalence}

\begin{definition}[Equivalence]
Two expressions are said to be syntactically equivalent or just
  \textbf{equivalent} (denoted by \(\equiv\)) if their normal forms are
  syntactically identical.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{sec:evaluation}

The \emph{evaluation} function
  \(\mathbb{E} : \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{S}
    \rightarrow \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{S} \)
  maps formations to normal forms
  by calling the function attached to $L$
  and possibly modifying the \emph{state} of evaluation:
\begin{phiquation*}
\mathbb{E}([[ B_1, L>F, B_2 ]], s_1) \rightarrow F([[ B_1, L>F, B_2 ]], s_1) \rightarrow \langle n, s_2 \rangle.
\end{phiquation*}

The function \(F\) is called by value.

\subsection{Morphing}\label{sec:morphing}

The \emph{morphing} function
  \(\mathbb{M} : \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{S}
    \rightarrow \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{S} \)
  maps normal forms to normal forms,
  possibly modifying the \emph{state} of evaluation.
The inference rules at \cref{fig:morphing} inductively describe
  the algorithm of morphing.

The morphing function is total, meaning that
  every normal form can be morphed in any state.
The inference rules of \cref{fig:morphing} cover all cases:
  for any \(n \in \mathcal{N}\) and any \(s \in \mathcal{S}\),
  a rule always applies and its derivation terminates,
  yielding a normal form and a state.
Thus, \(\mathbb{M}\) is defined for every pair of normal form and state.

\begin{mdframed}
\iexec[maybe]{phino explain --morph | sed 's/\\$/\\\\xi /g' > _tex/rules-morph.tex}
\inputrules{_tex/rules-morph.tex}
\end{mdframed}
\capt{Morphing rules.}
\label{fig:morphing}

The notation $<< n_1, e, s_1 >> \stepto << n_2, s_2 >>$
  means that \(\mathbb{M}(n_1, e, s_1)\) evaluates
  to \(n_2\), thus \emph{morphing} \(n_1\)
  with a side-effect of changing \(s_1\) to \(s_2\),
  having $Q$ equal to \(e\).

\subsection{Dataization}\label{sec:dataization}

The \emph{dataization} function
  \(\mathbb{D} : \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{S}
    \rightharpoonup \mathcal{D} \times \mathcal{S}\)
  maps normal forms to data, possibly modifying the \emph{state}
  of evaluation.
The inference rules in \cref{fig:dataization} inductively
  describe the algorithm of dataization.

\begin{figure*}
\begin{mdframed}
\iexec[maybe]{phino explain --dataize > _tex/rules-dataize.tex}
\inputrules{_tex/rules-dataize.tex}
\end{mdframed}
\capt{Dataization rules.}
\label{fig:dataization}
\end{figure*}

The notation $<< n, e, s_1 >> \stepto << \delta, s_2 >>$
  means that \(\mathbb{D}(n, e, s_1)\) evaluates to \(\delta\),
  thus \emph{dataizing} \(n\) with a side-effect of changing
  the state of evaluation \(s_1\) to \(s_2\),
  having $Q$ equal to \(e\).
The notation \(\mathbb{D}(n, u)\) is a shortened form
  of \(\mathbb{D}(n, u, s_0)_{(1)}\),
  which is a \emph{pure} dataization ---
  inputting an empty state of evaluation and ignoring
  the output state of evaluation.
Here and later, the notation \(x_{(i)}\) denotes the \(i\)-th
  element of the tuple \(x\) (counting starts from one).

\subsection{Congruence}

\begin{definition}[Congruence]
Two expressions \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are said to be behaviorally equivalent
  or \textbf{congruent} (denoted by \(\cong\)) if for any state \(s\)
  and any \(e\):
  \(\mathbb{D}(n_1, e, s) = \mathbb{D}(n_2, e, s)\).
\end{definition}

Two congruent expressions may be non-equivalent, for example:
\begin{phiquation*}
\Big\{ \tau_1 -> [[ foo -> ?, D> 01-02 ]], \tau_2 -> [[ bar -> ?, D> 01-02 ]] \Big\}
Q.\tau_1 \cong Q.\tau_2 \;\not\to\; Q.\tau_1 \equiv Q.\tau_2 {.}
\end{phiquation*}
